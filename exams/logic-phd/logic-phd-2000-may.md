# Logic, PhD exam, May 2000

*Answer 7 questions, including at least one from each of the four segments. Problems 1–4 concern General Logic; problems 5–7 concern Model Theory; problems 8–10 concern Set Theory; and problems 11–13 concern Recursion Theory.*

**1.** State Gödel’s incompleteness theorem, and explain the role of Gödel numbering in its proof.

**2.** Prove that there is no finite set \(\Gamma\) of sentences in the language of groups such that a group \(G\) satisfies all the sentences in \(\Gamma\) if and only if \(G\) is not torsion free. [The group \(G\) is torsion free if \(g^n \ne e\) for all \(g \ne e\) in \(G\).]

**3.** Show that any complete, finitely axiomatizable first-order theory is decidable.

**4.** Give an example of two models which are elementarily equivalent, but not isomorphic.

**5.** State and sketch a proof of the downward Löwenheim–Skolem theorem.

**6.** State the omitting types theorem, and use it to prove that any \(\omega\)-complete theory for arithmetic has an \(\omega\)-model.

**7.** State and sketch a proof of the Łoś theorem about ultraproducts of models.

**8.** Prove that a countable union of countable sets is countable.

**9.** Sketch the proof that \(L \models \mathrm{GCH}\).

**10.** Prove that if \(\kappa\) is a regular, uncountable cardinal then any diagonal intersection of closed and unbounded subsets of \(\kappa\) is closed and unbounded.

**11.** Show that a function \(h:\omega \to \omega\) is (partial) recursive if and only if its graph is recursively enumerable; and that if \(h\) is total then its graph is recursive.

**12.** State the halting problem, and prove that it is undecidable.

**13.** Explain why, for any \(A \subset \omega\), there are at most countably many sets \(B\) such that \(B <_T A\).
